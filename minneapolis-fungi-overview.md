# Fungi of Minneapolis — Overview

Minneapolis and the surrounding region sit at the intersection of several forest types, making it unusually rich in fungal diversity. Fungi are neither plant nor animal — they form their own kingdom, and the mushroom is only the visible tip: most of the organism is a vast underground network of fine threads (mycelium) that can permeate acres of soil and wood. Minnesota's mix of oak savannas, maple-basswood forests, river bottomlands, boggy wetlands, and disturbed urban green spaces supports a remarkable range of species.

> **A note on seasons:** Unlike wildflowers, fungi don't have a strict annual bloom calendar — fruiting is triggered by temperature and moisture. In Minnesota, the main windows are **spring** (April–June, after snowmelt), **midsummer** (July, following warm rains), and **fall** (August–October, the most productive season). Some species are highly predictable; others appear only in good moisture years.

## Mycorrhizal Fungi

Mycorrhizal fungi form symbiotic relationships with tree roots, exchanging minerals and water for sugars. They cannot survive without their host trees, so you find them predictably near specific species. Many of the most prized edible mushrooms are in this group.

**How to identify (as a group):** Mycorrhizal mushrooms grow from the soil near their host tree, never from dead wood. Learning the tree associations is often the fastest field ID shortcut — finding a bolete under a birch, or a russula under an oak, immediately narrows the options. Many have caps that are convex when young and flatten with age; gills or pores are usually pale and do not detach cleanly from the cap.

**Ecosystem role:** Mycorrhizal networks are sometimes called the "wood wide web." A single mature tree may be connected to dozens of fungal species simultaneously, and through those fungi to dozens of neighboring trees. Carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and water move through these networks. Trees under stress can receive resources from neighbors via fungal connections. The relationship is ancient — mycorrhizal associations appear in the fossil record 450 million years ago and were likely critical to the colonization of land by plants.

## Saprotrophic Fungi

Saprotrophic fungi decompose dead organic matter — fallen logs, leaf litter, buried wood, dung, and other debris. They are the primary agents of wood decay in forests, recycling the carbon and nutrients locked in cellulose and lignin.

**How to identify (as a group):** Saprotrophic mushrooms typically grow directly from dead wood, stumps, leaf litter, or disturbed soil. Many grow in clusters. Wood-rotting species leave characteristic patterns: **white rot** fungi (including oyster mushrooms) break down both cellulose and lignin, leaving pale, stringy wood; **brown rot** fungi break down cellulose but leave brown, crumbly, cubically cracked wood behind.

**Ecosystem role:** Without saprotrophic fungi, forests would choke in their own debris. The nutrients locked in dead wood and leaves would remain inaccessible to plants for centuries. Fungi are the primary decomposers of lignin — a tough polymer that almost nothing else can break down. The resulting nutrients feed the next generation of trees. Dead logs colonized by fungi also become habitat: softened wood is excavated by woodpeckers, then used by secondary cavity nesters; the fungi themselves are eaten by beetles, slugs, squirrels, and flying squirrels that cache pieces in tree cavities.

## Morels & Other Spring Fungi

Spring fungi emerge in the brief warm window before summer heat and dryness set in. Morels are the most celebrated spring mushroom in Minnesota — and among the most eagerly anticipated foraging events of the year.

**How to identify:** Morels are unmistakable once you know them — the cap is a honeycomb of deep pits and ridges, not smooth or gilled, and the entire mushroom is hollow when sliced vertically. True morels are hollow all the way through; false morels (_Gyromitra_) are chambered or stuffed with cottony fibers — an important distinction since false morels contain a toxic compound (gyromitrin) that can be deadly. Morel hunting is timed to the lilac bloom and soil temperature reaching ~50°F.

**Ecosystem role:** Morels have a complex and not fully understood ecology. Some species are saprotrophic and appear prolifically in areas disturbed by fire or tree death — a burn site can produce astonishing quantities of morels the following spring. Others are at least partially mycorrhizal with elms, ashes, and dying trees. Their spring emergence before the soil food web is fully active makes them an important early food source for insects, slugs, and small mammals. Morel spores are spread by animals, wind, and — increasingly studied — by the foragers who carry them on boots and baskets.

## Gilled Mushrooms (Miscellaneous Agarics)

The largest and most diverse group — gilled mushrooms span every habitat and ecological role. Many of the most dangerous species are here, alongside some of the best edibles. This section covers notable species that don't fit neatly into the categories above.

**How to identify:** Gilled mushrooms have blade-like gills on the cap underside that radiate from the stem. The key safety rule: **learn the deadly Amanitas first** (destroying angel, death cap, and the Galerina/Lepiota groups) before eating any gilled mushroom. Gill color, presence of a ring (annulus) on the stem, presence of a cup (volva) at the base, and spore print color are the critical ID features.

**Ecosystem role:** Gilled mushrooms occupy nearly every niche — mycorrhizal partners, saprotrophs, and even parasites. Many are important food sources for wildlife: white-tailed deer, squirrels, voles, and slugs eat mushrooms heavily. Flying squirrels cache dried fungi in tree cavities. The deer truffle (_Elaphomyces_), though rarely seen, is a hypogeous (underground) fungus heavily consumed by squirrels and small mammals who dig for them, inadvertently spreading spores. Meadow mushrooms and other open-ground species were an important food for Indigenous peoples and European settlers across the Upper Midwest.

## Boletes

Boletes are fleshy fungi with spongy pores on the cap underside instead of gills — one of the most distinctive and rewarding groups to learn. Most are mycorrhizal. Minnesota has outstanding bolete diversity in mixed forest.

**How to identify:** Flip the cap — if you see a spongy layer of small pores (tubes) rather than gills, it's a bolete. Key safety rule: **avoid any bolete with red or orange pore surface, or any that stains blue rapidly when cut** — these include several toxic species. The pore color and bluing reaction are the two most important safety checks in this group.

**Ecosystem role:** Boletes are among the most ecologically important mycorrhizal fungi in northern forests. Their mycelium extends far beyond the visible mushroom, forming networks with multiple tree species simultaneously. Squirrels and chipmunks heavily consume boletes and cache dried pieces in tree forks, spreading spores widely. Bolete pores are often colonized by fly larvae (maggots) within hours of fruiting — a reminder that the mushroom is not for the fungus's benefit but for spore dispersal, and that the forest ecosystem moves fast.

## Puffballs, Earthstars & Stinkhorns

A visually distinctive group united by unconventional fruiting body shapes — no caps and gills, but instead spheres, stars, cages, and tentacles.

**How to identify:** Puffballs are edible when the interior flesh is pure white and homogeneous throughout — slice them vertically to check. Any tinge of yellow, purple, or the outline of a developing mushroom inside means it is either overripe or an Amanita egg (which can look identical externally). Stinkhorns emerge from a white egg-like structure; their foul smell attracts flies that disperse spores. Earthstars start as puffballs, then the outer layer splits into star-shaped rays that lift the central spore sac off the ground.

**Ecosystem role:** Puffballs can release trillions of spores in a single puff — a giant puffball contains an estimated 7 trillion spores, more than enough to cover the Earth's land surface if each germinated. The stinkhorn's strategy of using carrion-smelling gleba (spore mass) to attract flies represents a fundamentally different dispersal strategy from wind-dispersed puffballs, and illustrates the diversity of solutions fungi have evolved for the same problem of moving spores across a landscape.

## Cup Fungi & Other Ascomycetes

A diverse group of fungi that release spores from microscopic sacs (asci) rather than from gills or pores. Includes the morels (covered above), as well as cups, saddles, truffles, and the familiar powdery mildews and yeasts.

**How to identify:** Most visible ascomycetes in this group have cup, saucer, or saddle-shaped fruiting bodies rather than the cap-and-stem form of most mushrooms. Colors range from brilliant scarlet to dull brown. Many are small — a hand lens helps. Look on wet decaying wood, damp soil, fallen nuts, and leaf litter.

**Ecosystem role:** Ascomycetes include some of the most ecologically critical fungi on Earth. Lichens — the crusty, leafy, or bushy organisms on tree bark and rocks — are stable partnerships between ascomycete fungi and algae or cyanobacteria; they are among the first colonizers of bare rock and produce acids that begin soil formation. Dutch elm disease and chestnut blight, two of the most ecologically catastrophic events in North American forest history, were both caused by invasive ascomycete fungi. On the positive side, ergot fungi on wild grasses were central to Indigenous ceremonial practices, and penicillin was discovered from an ascomycete mold.
