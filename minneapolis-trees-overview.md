# Trees of Minneapolis — Overview

Minneapolis sits in a transition zone between eastern deciduous forest and the Great Plains, giving it a rich mix of native species. The city itself has ~979,000 trees covering about 26% of its area.

---

## Conifers (Evergreens)

Most native conifers are more common in northern Minnesota but appear in parks and yards throughout Minneapolis.

### Pines
| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Eastern White Pine | *Pinus strobus* | Pinaceae | Pinales | Soft needles in bundles of 5; Minnesota's tallest native conifer |
| Red Pine | *Pinus resinosa* | Pinaceae | Pinales | State tree; orange-red upper bark; needles in bundles of 2 |
| Jack Pine | *Pinus banksiana* | Pinaceae | Pinales | Scrappy, small; poor sandy soils; short needles in bundles of 2 |

### Spruce & Fir
| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| White Spruce | *Picea glauca* | Pinaceae | Pinales | Square needles; common in northern/central MN |
| Black Spruce | *Picea mariana* | Pinaceae | Pinales | Similar; prefers wet areas |
| Balsam Fir | *Abies balsamea* | Pinaceae | Pinales | Flat, fragrant needles; Minnesota's only native fir |

### Cedar & Hemlock
| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Northern White Cedar | *Thuja occidentalis* | Cupressaceae | Pinales | Scale-like foliage; conifer swamps |
| Eastern Red Cedar | *Juniperus virginiana* | Cupressaceae | Pinales | Actually a juniper; scale-like foliage |
| Eastern Hemlock | *Tsuga canadensis* | Pinaceae | Pinales | Short soft needles; western edge of its range in MN |

### Special Case: Tamarack
| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tamarack | *Larix laricina* | Pinaceae | Pinales | Minnesota's only **deciduous** conifer; turns gold and drops needles in fall; loves bogs and wet soils |

## Deciduous Trees

### Maples *(Acer)*
The maple-basswood forest is the dominant native ecosystem in central/southeastern Minnesota.

| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sugar Maple | *Acer saccharum* | Sapindaceae | Sapindales | Classic fall color (scarlet, orange, yellow); sap for syrup |
| Red Maple | *Acer rubrum* | Sapindaceae | Sapindales | Red flowers in spring; variable fall color |
| Silver Maple | *Acer saccharinum* | Sapindaceae | Sapindales | Silvery leaf undersides; common along rivers |
| Black Maple | *Acer nigrum* | Sapindaceae | Sapindales | Very similar to sugar maple; eastern MN |
| Box Elder | *Acer negundo* | Sapindaceae | Sapindales | A maple with compound leaves — often mistaken for ash; one of the 3 most common urban trees in Minneapolis |
| Norway Maple *(introduced)* | *Acer platanoides* | Sapindaceae | Sapindales | European; widely planted as a boulevard tree |

### Oaks *(Quercus)*
Oaks split cleanly into two groups based on leaf shape.

**White Oaks** — rounded leaf lobes, acorns mature in 1 year
| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bur Oak | *Quercus macrocarpa* | Fagaceae | Fagales | Most widespread MN oak; distinctive fringed acorn cap |
| White Oak | *Quercus alba* | Fagaceae | Fagales | Large spreading crown; 7–10 evenly-lobed leaves |

**Red Oaks** — pointed, bristle-tipped leaf lobes, acorns mature in 2 years
| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Northern Red Oak | *Quercus rubra* | Fagaceae | Fagales | Fast-growing; common in parks and natural areas |
| Black Oak | *Quercus velutina* | Fagaceae | Fagales | Very dark, rough bark; southern MN |

### Ashes *(Fraxinus)*
Opposite compound leaves with 5–11 leaflets. Currently under severe pressure from **emerald ash borer**.

| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Green Ash | *Fraxinus pennsylvanica* | Oleaceae | Lamiales | One of the 3 most common urban trees in Minneapolis; adaptable |
| White Ash | *Fraxinus americana* | Oleaceae | Lamiales | Similar; slightly larger leaflets |

### Elms *(Ulmus)*
The American elm was once the dominant boulevard tree in Minneapolis (200,000–600,000 trees). Dutch elm disease reduced that to ~35,000.

| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| American Elm | *Ulmus americana* | Ulmaceae | Rosales | Classic vase shape; one of the 3 most common urban trees; rough-edged leaves |
| Slippery Elm | *Ulmus rubra* | Ulmaceae | Rosales | Similar habitat; fuzzy twigs and buds |
| Rock Elm | *Ulmus thomasii* | Ulmaceae | Rosales | Hardy; smaller than American elm |

### Birches *(Betula)*
Distinctive jagged-edged leaves; most species have notable bark.

| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Paper Birch | *Betula papyrifera* | Betulaceae | Fagales | White peeling bark; golden fall color; iconic MN species |
| Yellow Birch | *Betula alleghaniensis* | Betulaceae | Fagales | Aromatic twigs; mesic hardwood forests |
| River Birch | *Betula nigra* | Betulaceae | Fagales | Peeling salmon/tan bark; floodplains; increasingly planted in Twin Cities |

### Basswood / Linden *(Tilia)*
| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| American Basswood | *Tilia americana* | Malvaceae | Malvales | Large heart-shaped leaves; fragrant summer flowers beloved by bees; core of the maple-basswood forest |
| Little-leaf Linden *(introduced)* | *Tilia cordata* | Malvaceae | Malvales | European; popular urban street tree |

### Poplars & Aspens *(Populus)*
Fast-growing, short-lived, weak-wooded. Quaking aspen is the most abundant tree species in Minnesota.

| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Quaking Aspen | *Populus tremuloides* | Salicaceae | Malpighiales | Leaves flutter in the slightest breeze; golden fall color; found in every MN county |
| Bigtooth Aspen | *Populus grandidentata* | Salicaceae | Malpighiales | Similar to quaking; larger leaf teeth |
| Eastern Cottonwood | *Populus deltoides* | Salicaceae | Malpighiales | Minnesota's tallest native tree; large triangular leaves; river corridors |
| Balsam Poplar | *Populus balsamifera* | Salicaceae | Malpighiales | Aromatic buds; northern MN |

### Walnuts & Hickories
Compound leaves; valuable hardwoods; produce edible nuts.

| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Black Walnut | *Juglans nigra* | Juglandaceae | Fagales | Hard-shelled nuts; produces chemicals toxic to some plants |
| Butternut | *Juglans cinerea* | Juglandaceae | Fagales | Similar to walnut; less common |
| Shagbark Hickory | *Carya ovata* | Juglandaceae | Fagales | Distinctive shaggy, plated bark |
| Bitternut Hickory | *Carya cordiformis* | Juglandaceae | Fagales | Sulfur-yellow buds; less shaggy bark |

### Commonly Planted Non-Native Trees
| Common Name | Scientific Name | Family | Order | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Honeylocust | *Gleditsia triacanthos* | Fabaceae | Fabales | Fine, feathery compound leaves; thorny; widely planted after Dutch elm disease wiped out elms |
| Northern Catalpa | *Catalpa speciosa* | Bignoniaceae | Lamiales | Giant heart-shaped leaves; long bean-like pods; showy white flowers |
| Crabapple | *Malus* spp. | Rosaceae | Rosales | Spring flowers, small fruits; 120+ cultivars; very common ornamental |

## Where to See Them

| Location | What You'll Find |
|---|---|
| **Minneapolis streets & boulevards** | Green ash, American elm, box elder, Norway maple, honeylocust, crabapple, linden |
| **Chain of Lakes** (Harriet, Bde Maka Ska, Lake of the Isles) | Mature maples, ashes, oaks, basswood |
| **Mississippi River Corridor** | Cottonwood, river birch, silver maple, oak, ash; floodplain species |
| **Eloise Butler Wildflower Garden** | Native woodland species including bur oak, sugar maple, basswood |
| **Northern/Central MN forests** | Quaking aspen, paper birch, white pine, spruce, tamarack |
