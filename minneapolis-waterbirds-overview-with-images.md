# Water Birds of Minneapolis — Overview

Minneapolis is unusually rich in water bird habitat for a major city. The Chain of Lakes, Mississippi River gorge, Minnehaha Creek corridor, and dozens of smaller wetlands sit within the Mississippi Flyway — one of the four major North American migration corridors. The result is a city where you can reliably see loons, herons, mergansers, and a rotating cast of migrating shorebirds and waterfowl without leaving the urban core.

---

## Ducks & Geese (Waterfowl)

Waterfowl are the most visible water birds in Minneapolis — present year-round on the chain of lakes, and swelling dramatically during spring and fall migration.

**How to identify:** Ducks split into two main groups based on feeding behavior. **Dabbling ducks** feed at the surface, tipping tail-up to reach underwater — they take flight directly upward from the water. **Diving ducks** dive completely below the surface and must run along the water to get airborne. In most duck species, males (drakes) are more colorful; females are streaky brown. Geese are larger, longer-necked, and mostly brown or gray-white.

**Ecosystem role:** Waterfowl are ecosystem engineers on lakes and wetlands. Their feeding disturbs sediments and cycles nutrients from lake bottoms to the water column. Diving ducks help control aquatic invertebrate populations; dabbling ducks control aquatic vegetation. Canada geese, present year-round in large numbers, are a mixed ecological story: their grazing and waste significantly affect water quality in smaller urban lakes. On the positive side, migrating waterfowl connect distant wetlands by transporting seeds, invertebrates, and even small organisms on their feathers and in their digestive tracts.

## Mergansers

Mergansers are diving ducks with long, serrated bills designed to grip fish — they occupy a different niche than the filter-feeding or vegetation-grazing dabbling ducks.

**How to identify:** All three Minnesota mergansers have a distinctly narrow, serrated "sawbill" — completely unlike the broad flat bill of most ducks. Common and red-breasted mergansers are large; hooded merganser is small and round-headed. Hooded merganser's large fan-shaped crest is unmistakable — the male's is white and black, the female's is reddish-brown.

**Ecosystem role:** Mergansers are fish specialists and significant predators of small fish and aquatic invertebrates. Their presence is a rough indicator of water clarity and fish populations. Common mergansers nest in large tree cavities and are one of the cavity nesters that benefited from the proliferation of nest boxes. Hooded mergansers use nest boxes designed for wood ducks and are increasingly common on wooded urban lakes — a conservation success story. Female mergansers are known for "brood amalgamation" — multiple females sometimes merge their ducklings into a single large gang tended by one female.

## Grebes

Grebes look superficially like ducks but are not related — they're an ancient lineage with lobed (not webbed) toes, nearly no tail, and an unusual ability to control their buoyancy. Although, superficially, they resemble other diving birds such as loons and coots, they are most closely related to flamingos, as supported by morphological, molecular and paleontological data.

**How to identify:** Grebes sit low in the water and dive frequently. Their lobed toes (each toe has separate side flanges, rather than connected webbing) are visible up close. Most species have distinctive breeding plumage with colorful head markings that become dull gray-brown in winter. The horns or ear tufts of horned and eared grebes are the fastest field mark. Pied-billed grebe is small, brown, and thick-billed — it can sink slowly below the surface without diving.

**Ecosystem role:** Grebes eat fish, aquatic invertebrates, and — unusually — their own feathers. Adults feed feathers to their chicks and maintain a stomach full of them throughout their lives. The leading hypothesis is that feathers slow the passage of fish bones, allowing them to be digested rather than passed into the intestines where they could cause damage. Grebes are also notable for elaborate courtship displays — western and Clark's grebes perform a "rushing" display where paired birds run side-by-side across the water surface for up to 66 feet. Pied-billed grebes are important mid-level predators in urban wetlands.

## Herons & Egrets

Long-legged wading birds that stalk the shallows for fish, frogs, and invertebrates. Several species are a common sight along every Minneapolis lake and creek.

**How to identify:** Size and neck color are the two fastest ID marks. Great blue heron is enormous — nearly 4 feet tall, with a gray body, black eye stripe, and chestnut-brown neck. Great egret is white and nearly as tall. Black-crowned night-heron is stocky and compact, active at dusk. Green heron is small and dark, often hunched on a low branch over the water. In flight, all herons fold their neck into an S-curve — this immediately separates them from cranes, which fly with neck extended.

**Ecosystem role:** Herons are apex predators of the shallow-water food web. A great blue heron consumes several pounds of fish per day and can significantly influence small fish populations in urban ponds and lake shallows. They nest colonially in "heronries" — dense nesting aggregations in tall trees, often near water. Minneapolis and the surrounding area have several active heronries. Their colonial nesting has a downside: a disturbed heronry can lead to mass nest abandonment. Green herons are among the rare examples of tool use in birds — they have been observed dropping small objects (feathers, insects, crackers) onto the water surface as lures to attract fish.

## Loons & Cormorants

Deep divers that pursue fish underwater using their feet. Both groups are more common on larger, open lakes.

**How to identify:** Common loons are large, low-riding birds with a distinctive checkerboard black-and-white back in summer and a dagger-like bill. Their eerie wailing call is one of the iconic sounds of Minnesota lakes. Double-crested cormorants are similar in size but all black with an orange throat pouch; they often perch with wings spread to dry (they lack the waterproofing oils of ducks). Loons need a long running takeoff to become airborne; cormorants are more agile on takeoff.

**Ecosystem role:** Loons are the iconic Minnesota bird — the state bird — and a sensitive indicator of lake health. They need clear water to see fish while diving (they can reach 200 feet), undisturbed shoreline for nesting, and lakes largely free of powerboat traffic. Lead fishing tackle is a significant mortality source: loons swallow sinkers along with fish and die of lead poisoning. Double-crested cormorant populations declined severely due to DDT and were federally protected; they've rebounded dramatically since the 1970s — sometimes controversially, as their colonies can be very dense and destructive to nesting trees. Both species show that lakes are not isolated systems: the health of a lake is reflected in what lives on it.

## Rails, Coots & Gallinules

A group of wetland birds that are more often heard than seen — rails in particular are famously secretive. American coot is the exception: a bold, abundant, highly visible bird on nearly every Minneapolis lake.

**How to identify:** American coot looks like a dark, chunky duck but is not a duck — it's related to cranes. Its white chicken-like bill and lobed (not webbed) feet are distinctive. Rails are chicken-sized to sparrow-sized birds that skulk in cattails and dense marsh vegetation. The expression "thin as a rail" refers to their laterally compressed body, which lets them slip between reeds. You're far more likely to hear a sora's descending whinny or a Virginia rail's grunt than to see one.

**Ecosystem role:** Coots are important grazers of aquatic vegetation and can be present in flocks of hundreds during migration. They are aggressively territorial and will chase other waterfowl, creating spatial structure in how waterfowl use a lake. Rails are critical insectivores and seed consumers in marsh ecosystems, but because they're so secretive their ecological roles are poorly studied. Soras are one of the most abundant rails in North America despite being rarely seen. Yellow rails have one of the most unusual calls in North American birds — a series of clicking sounds resembling two stones tapped together — and nest in wet sedge meadows.

## Shorebirds

During spring and fall migration, mudflats and shallow water edges attract a rotating parade of shorebirds — sandpipers, yellowlegs, and dowitchers probing the mud for invertebrates.

**How to identify:** Shorebirds are notoriously challenging — many species look very similar, and their plumage changes between breeding, non-breeding, and juvenile. The most practical approach is to start with size and bill shape. Killdeer are large and have a distinctive double breast band. Yellowlegs are mid-sized with bright yellow legs. Dowitchers probe rapidly with a "sewing machine" motion. Spotted sandpiper bobs its tail constantly. Most shorebirds in Minneapolis are migrants stopping briefly on their way north or south.

**Ecosystem role:** Shorebirds are the connective tissue of wetland ecosystems across a hemisphere. A single bird that feeds on the mudflats of Lake Calhoun in August may have hatched on the Arctic tundra and be headed to a South American estuary. Their concentrated fall staging creates intense predation pressure on aquatic invertebrates at stopover sites, which stimulates invertebrate population turnover. Killdeer are year-round residents that nest on gravel and open ground throughout the city — they are famous for the broken-wing distraction display they perform to lure predators away from their nest.

## Kingfishers & Other Fishing Birds

A few species occupy the fishing niche from perches rather than from the water.

**How to identify:** Belted kingfisher is unmistakable — a chunky, big-headed, blue-gray bird with a ragged crest that perches over water and dives bill-first. Its rattling call carries far along creeks and lakeshores. Ospreys are large raptors (not technically water birds) but so consistently associated with water that they belong in any lake bird discussion — look for a large brown-and-white hawk hovering over open water before plunging feet-first.

**Ecosystem role:** Belted kingfisher is a keystone indicator species for stream and lake health — they require clear water for hunting and clean banks for nesting burrows. They excavate horizontal tunnels up to three feet into earthen banks to nest. Females are unusually more colorful than males (an exception to the typical bird pattern) — females have a rust-red belly band, males do not. Osprey populations crashed with DDT and have dramatically recovered — they now nest regularly around the Twin Cities and are a visible symbol of conservation success in the urban landscape.
