# Insects of Minneapolis — Overview

Minneapolis supports a surprisingly rich insect fauna for a major northern city. The Mississippi River corridor, chain of lakes, restored prairies, oak savannas, and even well-planted urban yards provide habitat for hundreds of species. Minnesota's hard winters act as a natural filter — the insects here are cold-adapted survivors, and the burst of activity from May through September is correspondingly intense.

Insects are the foundation of nearly every terrestrial food web. Songbirds, bats, frogs, fish, and most other animals depend on them directly or indirectly. Their decline — documented globally over the past several decades — is one of the most serious ecological trends of our time, making urban habitat all the more important.

> **A note on seasons:** Season columns reflect approximate adult activity windows in the Minneapolis area. Many species overwinter as eggs, larvae, or pupae and are active as adults for only a fraction of the year. Actual timing shifts with weather year to year.

## Butterflies

Butterflies are among the most visible and well-studied insects. Minneapolis sits in a rich zone for butterfly diversity — prairie species from the west overlap with forest species from the east, and the Mississippi corridor provides a migration pathway.

**How to identify:** Butterflies hold their wings vertically when resting (most of the time); moths typically hold them flat or tent-like. Beyond that, focus on wing pattern, size, and habitat. Many species have distinct upper and underwing patterns — the underwing is often what you see when the butterfly is perched. Caterpillar host plant association is a powerful secondary ID tool and explains why you find certain butterflies in certain places.

**Ecosystem role:** Butterflies are important pollinators, though generally less efficient than bees at pollen transfer. Their greater ecological value may be as prey — butterfly and moth caterpillars are among the most important food sources for nesting songbirds. A single clutch of chickadees requires roughly 6,000–9,000 caterpillars to fledge. Native plants support exponentially more caterpillar diversity than introduced plants, which is why urban plantings of native species matter so much. Monarch migration is one of the great spectacles of North American natural history — a multi-generational journey spanning thousands of miles that passes directly through Minneapolis each fall.

## Moths

Moths outnumber butterflies roughly 10 to 1 in species richness, but their mostly nocturnal habits make them far less familiar. Minnesota has over 2,000 moth species. Many are spectacular; nearly all are ecologically essential.

**How to identify:** Most moths fly at night and are attracted to lights. Resting posture varies — some hold wings flat, some tent them over the body, some roll them into a cylinder. Many have cryptic patterns that match tree bark or dead leaves. A moth sheet (white sheet with a blacklight) on a warm July or August night in Minneapolis can produce dozens of species. Wing span and hindwing color are key ID marks.

**Ecosystem role:** Moths are the dominant caterpillar contributors to forest food webs — far more so than butterflies, simply due to sheer numbers. Luna moths and cecropia moths are among the largest insects in North America, and their caterpillars are important food for cuckoos and other large insectivores. Sphinx moths (hummingbird moths) are important pollinators of deep-throated flowers including native orchids, phlox, and evening primrose — flowers whose nectar is inaccessible to most bees. Noctuid and geometrid moths — drab brown moths that most people never notice — are the most important caterpillar group for forest-nesting songbirds in Minnesota.

## Bees & Wasps

Minnesota has over 400 native bee species. Most are solitary ground-nesters or stem-nesters that most people have never noticed — they lack the yellow-and-black striping of bumblebees and are easily overlooked. They are irreplaceable as pollinators.

**How to identify:** Bees are generally hairier than wasps — pollen-collecting hairs on the legs or abdomen are definitive. Wasps have a narrow "wasp waist" and smooth, shinier bodies. Bumblebees are large and fuzzy; sweat bees are often tiny and metallic green; mining bees are mid-sized and fuzzy. Most native bees are solitary — they do not live in colonies and cannot sting unless physically handled.

**Ecosystem role:** Native bees are the primary pollinators of most native wildflowers, many of which co-evolved with specific bee species. Some plants require buzz pollination (sonication) — a technique where bees grip the flower and vibrate their flight muscles to shake loose pollen that is tightly packed in the anthers. Honeybees cannot do this; bumblebees can. Tomatoes, blueberries, and many native wildflowers depend on it. The decline of native bees — driven by habitat loss, pesticides, and disease — is one of the most serious threats to both wild ecosystems and agriculture. Yellow jackets and paper wasps, despite their reputation, are important predators of caterpillars and other insects, providing significant pest control services.

## Beetles

Beetles are the most species-rich order of insects — roughly one in four described animal species is a beetle. Minnesota has thousands of species. Many are cryptic, but several are large, colorful, and highly visible.

**How to identify:** The defining feature of beetles is hardened forewings (elytra) that meet in a straight line down the middle of the back, forming a protective shell over the hindwings. When a beetle flies, the elytra lift out of the way. Body shape varies enormously — round lady beetles, elongated longhorns, flat bark beetles, armored ground beetles. Many species are identified by the pattern on their elytra.

**Ecosystem role:** Beetles occupy nearly every ecological niche — predators, herbivores, detritivores, pollinators, parasites. Ground beetles are among the most important predators of soil invertebrates and slugs in gardens. Fireflies (technically beetles) are a beloved summer experience in Minneapolis and a sensitive indicator of moist, undisturbed habitat. Bark beetles are natural agents of forest disturbance — their outbreaks kill trees, creating snags and gaps that drive forest succession. The emerald ash borer, an introduced bark beetle, has been one of the most ecologically destructive invasive species in Minnesota history.

## Dragonflies & Damselflies

Odonates are among the most ancient insect lineages and among the most spectacular. Minneapolis's lakes, rivers, and wetlands support a rich odonate fauna. A summer walk along any lakeshore will turn up a dozen species.

**How to identify:** Dragonflies are larger and hold their wings horizontally when perched; damselflies are slender and fold their wings back along the body. Both have enormous compound eyes, two pairs of wings, and are aerial predators. In dragonflies, the hindwing is wider at the base than the forewing. Color, body pattern, and eye color are key ID marks. Many species are highly site-faithful — you can return to the same perch and find the same individual day after day.

**Ecosystem role:** Odonates are apex predators of the aquatic and aerial insect world. Larvae (nymphs) live underwater for 1–3 years, eating everything from mosquito larvae to small fish. Adults can consume hundreds of mosquitoes per day. A dense population of dragonflies over a lake is significant pest control. Their larvae are also important prey for fish, herons, and diving ducks. Because both larval and adult stages are sensitive to water quality and temperature, dragonflies are excellent bioindicators — their presence and diversity reflect lake and wetland health.

## Grasshoppers, Crickets & Katydids

The orthopterans are the sound-makers of the insect world. The chorus of crickets, katydids, and grasshoppers is one of the defining sounds of a Minneapolis late summer — and is entirely produced by males attracting mates.

**How to identify:** Grasshoppers have short antennae and are active by day; crickets and katydids have long antennae and are mostly nocturnal. Grasshoppers produce sound by rubbing hind legs against wings (stridulation) or by snapping wings in flight. Crickets and katydids rub their wings together. Each species has a unique call — learning calls is the most reliable way to identify many species.

**Ecosystem role:** Orthopterans are critical links in open-habitat food webs. Grasshoppers are major consumers of grasses and forbs — in years of high abundance, they can significantly affect prairie vegetation structure. They are equally important as prey: sparrows, meadowlarks, kestrels, and many other birds depend heavily on them. Crickets and katydids are important for bats — a big brown bat can consume hundreds of crickets per night. Field crickets also play a significant decomposer role, consuming dead plant material, fungi, and seeds.

## True Bugs & Other Hemiptera

"Bug" technically refers to this order — insects with piercing-sucking mouthparts and forewings that are half leathery, half membranous. Many important and familiar insects are in this group.

**How to identify:** True bugs have a distinctive X-pattern on their back where the wing tips overlap. They pierce plant or animal tissue to feed. Stink bugs, water striders, assassin bugs, and cicadas are all hemipterans. The triangular scutellum between the wings is often a useful ID mark.

**Ecosystem role:** Hemipterans include major plant feeders (aphids, leafhoppers, scales) and major predators (assassin bugs, ambush bugs, giant water bugs). Cicadas are ecologically remarkable — the periodical cicada broods that emerge in mass synchrony in eastern North America (not reaching MN, but nearby) are one of the great examples of predator satiation as an evolutionary strategy. Water striders are among the most visible insects on still water surfaces in Minneapolis, where their ability to walk on water makes them accessible even to casual observers.
